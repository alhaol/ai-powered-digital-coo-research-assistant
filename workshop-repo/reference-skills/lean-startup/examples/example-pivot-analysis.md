# Pivot-or-Persevere Analysis — Kinsight

## Hypothesis Under Review
"We believe parents of 11-17 year olds will pay a monthly subscription for an AI risk
score that tells them how their teen is really doing."

## Evidence Summary
| Source | Key Finding | Supports Hypothesis? |
|--------|-------------|---------------------|
| Parent survey (n=50) | 76% would not accept an algorithmic score about their child; only 12% rank a score as their top need | ❌ |
| Parent survey (n=50) | 64% say their top need is knowing what to say | ✅ (new direction) |
| Parent survey (n=50) | 56% would pay $5-10/mo; only 14% would pay $20+ | ⚠️ (shallow ceiling) |
| Clinician interviews (n=20) | 85% name intake & documentation as their top pain; 70% would pay per seat | ✅ (new direction) |
| Clinician interviews (n=20) | 0/20 get structured between-session signal today | ✅ (white space) |
| Competitive scan (5 platforms) | 3/5 already ship an AI score or risk flag; 0/5 connect parent to clinician | ❌ (lever), ✅ (gap) |

## Recommendation
**Decision:** ZOOM IN

The broad thesis — the gap between parents and clinicians is real and both sides feel it
— holds. The specific lever is wrong on three independent axes at once: customers reject
it (76%), competitors have already commoditized it (3/5), and it's the one feature that
can't be validated without clinical trials.

## Revised Hypothesis
"We believe child & adolescent clinicians have no structured picture of what happened
between sessions and will pay a per-seat licence if we offer an intake summary built
from the parent's own observations — and that parents will sustain those observations
because they get back a conversation guide, not a score."

Note the payer moved. Parent willingness is real but shallow ($5-10/mo, 56%); a clinic
seat is worth many times that and 70% said yes. Parents get the guide free through their
clinician, which is what keeps them checking in, which is what makes the summary worth
buying.

## Risk Assessment
Stated preference ≠ sustained behaviour. 64% *say* they want conversational help; nobody
has watched them log observations for six straight weeks. Leading indicator: day-14
retention in a 10-family manual pilot. Under 50% and the parent side can't feed the
clinician side, and the business doesn't work.

Secondary risk: clinicians may not trust parent-reported input near a chart. Mitigate by
keeping every summary editable before it's saved — the clinician stays the author.

## Confidence Level: High
Three independent signals agree, and the revised hypothesis reduces risk on every front:
it drops the regulated feature, moves to a payer who said yes, and narrows the build.

> **Note for the advisor:** a 50-person survey is a market signal, not a clinical
> finding. It can tell you nobody wants a score. It cannot tell you whether a score would
> work — that needs clinical validation and likely regulatory review. Both roads lead
> away from building it, but for different reasons, and a good recommendation says so.
