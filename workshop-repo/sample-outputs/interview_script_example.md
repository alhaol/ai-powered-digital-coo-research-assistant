## Kinsight — Parent Discovery Interview (10 questions, ~25 min)

**Hypothesis under test:** Parents of 11-17 year olds will pay for an AI risk score.
**Who you're interviewing:** Parents. **Not** their children.

> **Before you start.** You're a founder, not a clinician. If a parent discloses
> something serious, stop the interview and point them at real help. Never ask a parent
> to narrate their child's symptoms to validate a feature.

### Block 1 — Warm-Up (build context, no pitching)
1. Tell me about the last few months with your kid. What's felt different?
2. Who else is involved right now — a counsellor, a GP, the school, nobody yet?

### Block 2 — Pain Points (what actually happened)
3. Walk me through the last time you were genuinely worried. What did you do first?
4. What did you search for that night? What words did you actually type?
5. When you tried to talk to them about it, how did that conversation go?

### Block 3 — Current Alternatives (what they already tried)
6. What have you tried so far — apps, books, courses, calling someone? What happened to
   each one?
7. If you're seeing a clinician: what happens between appointments? How do they find out
   what changed?

### Block 4 — Willingness to Pay (behaviour, not opinion)
8. Have you ever paid for anything to help with this? What was it, and what did it cost?
9. What would have to be true for you to pay $10 a month for something here?
10. If I built the wrong thing, what would it be? What would make you delete it in week
    one?

---

## Why These Questions

**Q3, Q4, Q5 are the spine.** They ask what the parent *did*, in the past, with specifics.
"What did you type into Google at 2am" is a fact. "Would you use a mood tracker?" is a
prediction, and parents are bad at predicting — as is everyone.

**Q10 is the most valuable question in the script.** Asking people to argue *against* your
product is the only reliable way to get a worried parent to stop being polite.

**Notice what's missing.** There is no question that says "would you like to know if your
child is at risk?" Every parent says yes to that. It measures nothing except how frightened
they are, and it's the exact question that would have confirmed Ahmad's wrong bet.

## The Mom Test Applied Here
| ❌ Don't ask | ✅ Ask instead |
|---|---|
| "Would you use an app that scores your teen's mood?" | "What did you do the last time you were worried?" |
| "Would a risk score be helpful?" | "What would you have wanted to know that night?" |
| "Do you think parents need this?" | "What have you already paid for, and did it help?" |
| "Would you pay $10/month?" | "What's the last thing you paid for to help with this?" |
