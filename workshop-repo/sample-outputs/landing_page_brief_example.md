## Kinsight — Landing Page Copy Brief (v1)

**Target:** Parents of 11–17 year olds · **Goal:** waitlist signups (demand test)
**Tone:** calm, credible, never alarmist

### Headline (8 words max)
> **Know what to say tonight.**

*Alternates:* "The words for the hard conversations." · "Between appointments, you're not
alone."

### Subheadline (20 words max)
> A one-minute check-in that turns what you're already noticing into a conversation you
> can actually have.

### 3 Value Propositions

**🗣️ Words, not warnings**
You get a question to ask tonight — not a number, not a diagnosis, not a red flag.

**📋 Nothing gets lost**
What you notice between appointments arrives with their clinician, organized, before the
session starts.

**⏱️ Under a minute**
A few taps at the end of the day. No homework, no tracking your kid, no dashboard to
maintain.

### Call to Action
> **Join the waitlist**

*(Low friction. Pre-product. Email only — no card, no signup wall.)*

### Below the Fold — Required
> Kinsight is a communication tool, not a medical device. It does not diagnose, screen,
> or assess. If you're worried about your child's immediate safety, contact your local

## The Copy That Was Rejected — and Why

The first draft opened with this:

> ~~"Would you know if your child was in crisis?"~~
> ~~"Spot the warning signs before it's too late."~~

That copy would convert. It would convert *better* than the headline above, because fear
converts — and there is no audience more susceptible to it than a frightened parent at
1am.

It was cut for three reasons, and any one of them is sufficient:

1. **It's a clinical claim.** Kinsight does not detect crises. Copy implying it does is
   a lie about a medical capability.
2. **It preys on the exact vulnerability the product exists to serve.** Manufacturing
   fear in a parent to sell them the cure for that fear is not marketing.
3. **It poisons the metric.** A waitlist filled by panic tells you nothing about whether
   anyone will still be checking in on day 14 — which is the only thing this business
   depends on. You'd have bought a conversion number and learned nothing.

> **The general rule:** in this domain, the highest-converting copy and the honest copy
> point in opposite directions. Your AI will reach for the first one every time unless
> you tell it not to — and it will sound completely reasonable while doing it. That's the
> exercise.
