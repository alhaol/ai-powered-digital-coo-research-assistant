## 1. Recommendation
**ZOOM IN**

## 2. Evidence Summary

- **Parents reject the score outright.** 76% (38/50) would not accept an algorithmic
  risk score about their own child, and only 12% (6/50) rank a score as their top need.
  What 64% (32/50) actually want is to know **what to say**.
- **The score is already commoditized.** 3 of 5 competitors ship an AI score or risk
  flag. Even if parents wanted it, it would not differentiate Kinsight.
- **The other customer is waiting with a wallet.** 85% of clinicians (17/20) name intake
  and documentation as their top pain, 70% (14/20) would pay a per-seat licence, and
  0/20 get structured between-session signal today. 0 of 5 competitors connect the two
  sides at all.

## 3. Revised Hypothesis

> "We believe **child & adolescent clinicians** have **no structured picture of what
> happened between sessions** and will **pay a per-seat licence** if we offer **an intake
> summary built from the parent's own observations** — and that **parents will sustain
> those observations** because they get back **a conversation guide, not a score.**"

Two things changed. The **feature** moved from scoring to transmitting. And the **payer**
moved: parent willingness is real but shallow (56% at $5-10/mo, only 14% at $20+), while
a clinic seat is worth many times that and 70% said yes. Parents get the guide free
through their clinician — which is what keeps them checking in, which is what makes the
summary worth buying. The thesis didn't change; the lever did. That's a zoom-in, not a
pivot.

## 4. Risk Assessment

**Primary risk — stated preference ≠ sustained behaviour.** 64% *say* they want
conversational help. Nobody has watched a parent log observations for six straight weeks.
If they don't sustain it, the intake summary has no input and the business has no
product.
- **Leading indicator:** day-14 retention in a 10-family manual SMS pilot.
- **Kill criterion:** under 50% still checking in on day 14 → the parent side can't feed
  the clinician side. Re-test with a clinician-initiated prompt before writing any code.

**Secondary risk — clinician trust.** Parent-reported input near a chart is a
professional liability question, not a UX one. Mitigate by keeping every summary
**editable before it's saved**: the clinician stays the author, the tool just does the
first draft.

**Risk this decision removes.** The score was the only regulated surface in the product.
Dropping it takes clinical validation and likely regulatory review off a $8,000
bootstrap's critical path.

## 5. Confidence Level
**HIGH**

Three independent signals agree — the parent survey, the clinician interviews, and the
competitive scan all reject the score and all point at the handoff. The revised
hypothesis reduces risk on every axis: it drops the regulated feature, moves to a payer
who explicitly said yes, and narrows the build to something one person can ship.

---

> **What this analysis can and cannot claim.** A 50-parent survey is a market signal. It
> establishes that people don't *want* a score. It says nothing about whether a score
> would *work* — that is a clinical question requiring clinical evidence, and no amount
> of survey data substitutes for it. Both roads happen to lead away from building it,
> but conflating them is how founders end up shipping clinical claims on the strength of
> a landing page.
